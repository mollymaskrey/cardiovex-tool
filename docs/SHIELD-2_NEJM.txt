ABSTRACT
Background
The SHIELD-1 trial demonstrated that cardiovexaban, a dual P2Y12 and PAR-1 inhibitor, reduced ma-
jor adverse cardiovascular and cerebrovascular events (MACCE) in high-risk patients with atheroscle-
rotic cardiovascular disease (ASCVD). SHIELD-2 was designed as a confirmatory trial to validate
these findings in an independent population.
Methods
In this multicenter, randomized, double-blind, placebo-controlled trial, we enrolled 7,842 patients with
documented ASCVD and elevated residual cardiovascular risk (10-year ASCVD risk score ≥15% or
≥2 high-risk features) despite guideline-directed medical therapy. Patients were randomly assigned
1:1 to cardiovexaban 150 mg once daily or placebo. The primary endpoint was time to first occurrence
of MACCE (cardiovascular death, myocardial infarction, stroke, or urgent coronary revascularization).
Key secondary endpoints included three-point MACE (cardiovascular death, MI, or stroke), individual
MACCE components, and stroke specifically.
Results
Over a median follow-up of 36 months, MACCE occurred in 449 patients (11.5%) in the cardiovexaban
group versus 590 patients (15.1%) in the placebo group (hazard ratio, 0.73; 95% confidence interval
[CI], 0.63 to 0.85; P<0.001). Three-point MACE occurred in 372 patients (9.5%) versus 503 patients
(12.9%) (HR, 0.71; 95% CI, 0.59 to 0.85; P<0.001). Stroke occurred in 96 patients (2.5%) versus
142 patients (3.6%) (HR, 0.68; 95% CI, 0.51 to 0.89; P=0.006). TIMI major bleeding occurred in 160
patients (4.1%) versus 90 patients (2.3%) (HR, 1.77; 95% CI, 1.35 to 2.33; P<0.001), predominantly
gastrointestinal. Intracranial hemorrhage rates were balanced (0.4% vs. 0.3%).
Conclusions
SHIELD-2 confirmed the findings of SHIELD-1, demonstrating that cardiovexaban reduces MACCE
in high-risk ASCVD patients on guideline-directed therapy, with particular benefit on stroke preven-
tion. Major bleeding was increased but driven primarily by manageable gastrointestinal events.

SHIELD-2 Trial — Cardiovexaban Confirmatory Study 2
1 I NTRODUCTION
The SHIELD-1 trial established that cardiovexaban—a novel dual-pathway antiplatelet agent inhibiting both
P2Y12 and protease-activated receptor-1 (PAR-1)—reduces major adverse cardiovascular and cerebrovas-
cular events (MACCE) by 25% in high-risk patients with established atherosclerotic cardiovascular disease
(ASCVD) receiving guideline-directed medical therapy (GDMT). The consistent benefit across all compo-
nents of the composite endpoint, coupled with manageable bleeding risk driven primarily by gastrointestinal
events rather than intracranial hemorrhage, suggested a favorable benefit-risk profile in appropriately se-
lected patients.
However, replication of cardiovascular outcomes trials in independent populations is critical for estab-
lishing treatment effects as robust and generalizable. Historical examples—including early fibrinolytic trials,
glycoprotein IIb/IIIa inhibitor studies, and more recent investigations of novel antiplatelet strategies—have
demonstrated that initial positive findings are not always reproduced in confirmatory studies, particularly
when baseline risk profiles, background therapies, or event adjudication methods differ between trials.
The SHIELD-2 trial was designed as a confirmatory study to validate the MACCE reduction observed
with cardiovexaban in SHIELD-1, using identical eligibility criteria, treatment protocols, and endpoint def-
initions but enrolling an independent patient population at different sites with different investigators. Addi-
tionally, SHIELD-2 prospectively specified stroke as a key secondary endpoint, given the particularly robust
32% reduction observed in SHIELD-1 and the recognition that PAR-1 inhibition may have preferential ef-
fects on thrombotic stroke through mechanisms distinct from coronary thrombosis.
A unique aspect of SHIELD-2 was its conduct during a different phase of the COVID-19 pandemic
compared with SHIELD-1. While SHIELD-1 enrolled primarily during the early pandemic period (March
2021 to November 2022), SHIELD-2 enrolled during a later phase (September 2022 to June 2023) when
vaccination rates were higher and COVID-related healthcare disruptions had largely normalized. This pro-
vided an opportunity to assess whether the treatment effect of cardiovexaban was robust across different
baseline event rate environments.
2 M ETHODS
2.1 Trial Design and Oversight
SHIELD-2 was a multicenter, randomized, double-blind, placebo-controlled trial conducted at 387 sites
in 26 countries. The trial design was nearly identical to SHIELD-1, with enrollment criteria, treatment
protocols, and endpoint definitions harmonized to maximize comparability. The protocol was approved by
institutional review boards or ethics committees at all sites. All patients provided written informed consent.
An independent data and safety monitoring board reviewed safety data at prespecified intervals. An
independent clinical endpoint adjudication committee, blinded to treatment assignments, adjudicated all
efficacy and safety endpoints using the same standardized definitions employed in SHIELD-1.
2.2 Patient Population
Eligibility criteria were identical to SHIELD-1. Patients were required to have documented ASCVD (prior
MI >30 days, prior ischemic stroke >90 days, or symptomatic PAD) and elevated residual risk defined as
either 10-year ASCVD risk score ≥15% or ≥2 high-risk features (diabetes with target organ damage, CKD
stage 3, or recurrent events within 24 months).
All patients were required to be on stable GDMT including antiplatelet therapy (aspirin 75-100 mg daily
or clopidogrel 75 mg daily), statin therapy (high-intensity or maximally tolerated with LDL <100 mg/dL),
and blood pressure control (systolic BP <140 mm Hg if tolerated). Patients with diabetes required HbA1c
<9.0%.
Exclusion criteria matched SHIELD-1: planned revascularization within 3 months, active bleeding,

history of intracranial hemorrhage, severe heart failure, severe renal impairment (eGFR <30 ml/min/1.73
m2), need for oral anticoagulation, or prior intolerance to P2Y12 inhibitors.
2.3 Randomization and Treatment
Patients were randomly assigned 1:1 to cardiovexaban 150 mg once daily or matching placebo using a cen-
tral interactive web-response system. Randomization was stratified by geographic region, index qualifying
event, and background antiplatelet therapy.
Trial visits occurred at 1 month, 3 months, and every 3 months thereafter. Medication adherence was
assessed by pill counts. Background GDMT was continued and adjusted per standard of care.
2.4 Endpoints
The primary efficacy endpoint was time to first MACCE: cardiovascular death, myocardial infarction, stroke,
or urgent coronary revascularization. All endpoint definitions were identical to SHIELD-1.
Key secondary endpoints, tested hierarchically, included: (1) three-point MACE (CV death, MI, or
stroke); (2) stroke; (3) CV death; (4) myocardial infarction; (5) all-cause mortality.
The primary safety endpoint was TIMI major bleeding. Additional safety endpoints included TIMI
minor bleeding, intracranial hemorrhage, gastrointestinal bleeding, and treatment discontinuation due to
bleeding.
2.5 Statistical Analysis
SHIELD-2 was powered to detect a 20% relative risk reduction in MACCE with 90% power at alpha 0.05,
requiring approximately 1,050 events. Sample size was calculated assuming a 5.5% annual event rate in the
placebo group, similar to SHIELD-1.
The primary analysis used a Cox proportional-hazards model stratified by randomization factors in the
intention-to-treat population. Cumulative incidence was estimated using Kaplan-Meier methods. Hierarchi-
cal testing controlled type I error for secondary endpoints.
A prespecified exploratory analysis pooled SHIELD-1 and SHIELD-2 data to provide more precise
estimates of treatment effects, particularly for lower-frequency endpoints such as cardiovascular death and
intracranial hemorrhage.

3 R ESULTS
3.1 Patients
Between September 2022 and June 2023, 7,842 patients were enrolled and randomly assigned to cardiovex-
aban (3,921 patients) or placebo (3,921 patients). Baseline characteristics were well balanced (Table 1) and
similar to SHIELD-1. Mean age was 66.8 years, 29.4% were women, and 76.8% were white. The index
qualifying event was prior MI in 56.8%, prior stroke in 25.9%, and PAD in 17.3%. Median 10-year ASCVD
risk was 22.6% (IQR 17.3-30.1), with 65.1% having risk ≥20%. Diabetes was present in 63.4%, CKD in
42.1%, and prior coronary revascularization in 69.8%.
Median follow-up was 36.0 months (IQR 30.5-40.3). Adherence to trial medication at 36 months was
89.8% in the cardiovexaban group and 90.4% in placebo. Permanent discontinuation occurred in 17.9% of
cardiovexaban patients (8.8% due to adverse events, 3.6% due to bleeding) and 13.8% of placebo patients
(4.9% due to adverse events, 1.1% due to bleeding).
3.2 Primary Efficacy Endpoint
MACCE occurred in 449 patients (11.5%) in the cardiovexaban group versus 590 patients (15.1%) in the
placebo group (HR 0.73; 95% CI 0.63-0.85; P<0.001) (Table 2, Figure 1). The absolute risk reduction was
3.6 percentage points, with a number needed to treat of 28 patients for 36 months.
Kaplan-Meier curves began separating at approximately 4 months and continued to diverge throughout
follow-up. The annualized MACCE rate was 4.0% with cardiovexaban versus 5.3% with placebo (rate ratio
0.75; 95% CI 0.67-0.84).
3.3 Secondary Efficacy Endpoints
Three-point MACE (CV death, MI, or stroke) occurred in 372 patients (9.5%) versus 503 patients (12.9%)
(HR 0.71; 95% CI 0.59-0.85;P<0.001). This 29% relative risk reduction was consistent with the SHIELD-1
finding (HR 0.77).
Individual components showed directionally consistent benefit:
• Stroke: 96 patients (2.5%) vs. 142 patients (3.6%) (HR 0.68; 95% CI 0.51-0.89; P=0.006). This
represented a 32% relative risk reduction, identical to SHIELD-1.
• Cardiovascular death: 134 patients (3.4%) vs. 169 patients (4.3%) (HR 0.79; 95% CI 0.61-1.03;
P=0.08).
• Myocardial infarction: 186 patients (4.7%) vs. 253 patients (6.5%) (HR 0.73; 95% CI 0.59-0.91;
P=0.004).
• Urgent coronary revascularization: 81 patients (2.1%) vs. 98 patients (2.5%) (HR 0.83; 95% CI
0.61-1.12; P=0.22).
All-cause mortality occurred in 187 patients (4.8%) versus 227 patients (5.8%) (HR 0.82; 95% CI 0.67-
1.01; P=0.06).
3.4 Stroke Subtype Analysis
A prespecified exploratory analysis examined stroke subtypes. Among the 96 strokes in the cardiovexaban
group, 82 (85%) were ischemic, 9 (9%) were hemorrhagic, and 5 (5%) were undetermined. In the placebo
group, 129 of 142 strokes (91%) were ischemic, 8 (6%) hemorrhagic, and 5 (4%) undetermined.
The reduction in ischemic stroke was significant (HR 0.64; 95% CI 0.47-0.86), while hemorrhagic stroke
rates were numerically higher but not significantly different (HR 1.13; 95% CI 0.44-2.87; P=0.80).

3.5 Safety Endpoints
TIMI major bleeding occurred in 160 patients (4.1%) in the cardiovexaban group versus 90 patients (2.3%)
in the placebo group (HR 1.77; 95% CI 1.35-2.33; P<0.001) (Table 3). The absolute increase was 1.8
percentage points (number needed to harm = 56).
Gastrointestinal bleeding accounted for 112 events (70%) in the cardiovexaban group and 61 events
(68%) in placebo. Intracranial hemorrhage occurred in 15 patients (0.4%) receiving cardiovexaban and 11
patients (0.3%) receiving placebo (HR 1.36; 95% CI 0.63-2.95; P=0.44). Fatal bleeding occurred in 11
patients (0.3%) versus 7 patients (0.2%) (P=0.48).
TIMI minor bleeding occurred in 293 patients (7.5%) versus 172 patients (4.4%) (HR 1.71; 95% CI
1.40-2.08). Bleeding requiring transfusion occurred in 184 patients (4.7%) versus 102 patients (2.6%) (HR
1.80; 95% CI 1.41-2.30).
3.6 Pooled Analysis of SHIELD-1 and SHIELD-2
A prespecified pooled analysis of 16,048 patients from both trials showed:
• MACCE: HR 0.74 (95% CI 0.66-0.82; P<0.001)
• Three-point MACE: HR 0.74 (95% CI 0.65-0.84; P<0.001)
• Stroke: HR 0.68 (95% CI 0.56-0.83; P<0.001)
• CV death: HR 0.78 (95% CI 0.65-0.94; P=0.009)
• TIMI major bleeding: HR 1.80 (95% CI 1.48-2.18; P<0.001)
• Intracranial hemorrhage: HR 1.29 (95% CI 0.76-2.20; P=0.35)
The pooled analysis demonstrated cardiovascular death reduction reaching statistical significance with
narrower confidence intervals, and confirmed that intracranial hemorrhage risk was not significantly elevated
despite the larger sample size.

4 D ISCUSSION
SHIELD-2 successfully confirmed the primary findings of SHIELD-1, demonstrating a 27% relative risk
reduction in MACCE with cardiovexaban in high-risk ASCVD patients receiving guideline-directed ther-
apy. The consistency of results across two independent trials—conducted at different sites, during different
time periods, with different investigators—provides strong evidence that the treatment effect is robust and
reproducible.
Several aspects of the SHIELD-2 results merit emphasis. First, the magnitude of MACCE reduction
(HR 0.73) was nearly identical to SHIELD-1 (HR 0.75), with overlapping confidence intervals, indicating
highly consistent efficacy. The absolute risk reduction (3.6 percentage points vs. 3.8 percentage points) and
number needed to treat (28 vs. 26) were also similar, supporting the generalizability of the benefit.
Second, the stroke reduction observed in SHIELD-2 (HR 0.68, 32% relative risk reduction) precisely
replicated the SHIELD-1 finding. This consistency across trials is particularly noteworthy given that stroke
was a prespecified key secondary endpoint in SHIELD-2 but not hierarchically tested in SHIELD-1. The
stroke subtype analysis demonstrated that the benefit was driven by ischemic stroke prevention (HR 0.64),
with no significant increase in hemorrhagic stroke despite the mechanism of action involving platelet inhi-
bition.
The biologic rationale for cardiovexaban’s particularly robust effect on stroke relates to the distinct role
of PAR-1 in cerebrovascular thrombosis. While both coronary and cerebral thrombosis involve platelet acti-
vation, in vitro studies suggest that thrombin-mediated platelet activation via PAR-1 plays a disproportion-
ate role in cerebral microvascular thrombosis compared with coronary thrombosis, where ADP-mediated
P2Y12 activation may be more dominant. Dual-pathway inhibition appears to provide additive benefit in
the cerebrovascular bed, consistent with the 32% stroke reduction observed in both SHIELD trials.
Third, the three-point MACE endpoint (excluding urgent revascularization) showed a 29% reduction in
SHIELD-2, more pronounced than the 23% reduction observed in SHIELD-1. This suggests that the hard
cardiovascular outcomes—death, MI, and stroke—may benefit more from dual-pathway inhibition than the
softer endpoint of urgent revascularization, which is more susceptible to practice variation and adjudication
differences across sites.
The safety profile in SHIELD-2 closely mirrored SHIELD-1. TIMI major bleeding was increased by 1.8
percentage points (HR 1.77), with 70% of events being gastrointestinal. Critically, intracranial hemorrhage
rates remained low and balanced between groups (0.4% vs. 0.3%), and the pooled analysis of 16,048 patients
confirmed no significant ICH signal (HR 1.29; 95% CI 0.76-2.20). This finding is particularly reassuring
given that intracranial hemorrhage is the most feared complication of antiplatelet therapy and the endpoint
that most strongly influences clinical decision-making.
The net clinical benefit—balancing MACCE reduction against bleeding risk—favored cardiovexaban
in both trials. In SHIELD-2, for every 56 patients treated for 36 months, one major bleeding event was
caused, but for every 28 patients treated, one MACCE event was prevented. This 2:1 benefit-to-harm ratio
is consistent with SHIELD-1 and supports the use of cardiovexaban in high-risk populations where the
baseline MACCE risk justifies the bleeding trade-off.
The pooled analysis of SHIELD-1 and SHIELD-2 provided several important insights. First, the car-
diovascular death reduction reached statistical significance (HR 0.78; 95% CI 0.65-0.94; P=0.009), an out-
come that was directionally favorable but not significant in either trial individually due to lower event rates.
Second, the stroke benefit was highly robust (HR 0.68; 95% CI 0.56-0.83), with the upper bound of the
confidence interval excluding no effect. Third, the pooled intracranial hemorrhage analysis definitively
demonstrated no significant increase in this catastrophic outcome, despite doubling the sample size and
improving precision.
One notable difference between SHIELD-1 and SHIELD-2 relates to the pandemic environment. SHIELD-
1 enrolled during the early COVID-19 period when social distancing, reduced healthcare utilization, and

lower community respiratory infection rates may have suppressed cardiovascular event rates. SHIELD-
2 enrolled during a later phase when vaccination was widespread and healthcare systems had normalized.
Despite these different baseline environments, the treatment effect was consistent, suggesting that cardiovex-
aban’s benefit is not contingent on specific background event rate conditions.
The trial has limitations. The population was predominantly white and male, though racial and gender
representation was similar to other contemporary cardiovascular outcomes trials. Background antiplatelet
therapy was limited to aspirin or clopidogrel; efficacy and safety with ticagrelor or prasugrel were not
evaluated. Patients with severe renal impairment, need for oral anticoagulation, and recent acute coronary
syndrome were excluded. Median follow-up of 36 months may not capture very long-term outcomes.
In conclusion, SHIELD-2 confirmed that cardiovexaban reduces MACCE in high-risk ASCVD patients
on guideline-directed therapy, with particularly robust stroke prevention and an acceptable bleeding profile
characterized by gastrointestinal events but not intracranial hemorrhage. The replication of findings across
two large, independent trials provides strong evidence supporting the use of cardiovexaban in appropriately
selected high-risk secondary prevention populations.
